Where I’m Coming From

Professionally, I work at Fannie Mae in HR Risk/Governance, where I’ve spent my time thinking in systems: how policies get implemented, how controls hold up under pressure, how organizations manage risk at scale, and how to build repeatable processes that don’t break when the environment changes.

In parallel, I serve as an Intelligence Officer in the Air National Guard. That role reinforces discipline, accountability, and planning — but it also trains a specific kind of thinking: analyze messy information, form a clear picture, make decisions with limited time, and execute.

Why Software Engineering

I didn’t come into software through a traditional computer science track. I came into it because I’m wired to solve problems and build systems that work — and because I’ve seen firsthand how broken experiences can hit families when they’re already carrying enough.

My long-term “why” is simple: I want to build software that reduces friction for people, especially in healthcare experiences where time, accuracy, and empathy matter. I’m particularly interested in the real-world impact of reliable systems — like electronic health record workflows that don’t fail the people who need them most.

Family, Motivation, and Grit

I’m a husband and a dad. My wife, Mary, and I met through West Virginia University band, and we’ve built a full life together. We have four daughters, and being a father changes how you measure success — it stops being about what sounds impressive and becomes about what creates stability, opportunity, and a better future for your family.

Part of my drive comes from navigating hard situations that don’t care about your plans. It forced me to level up my discipline and follow-through. When I commit to learning something, I’m not doing it casually — I’m doing it to win.

My Software Engineering Path So Far

I’m currently enrolled in West Virginia University’s Online Master of Science in Software Engineering (MSSE). I completed:

  • SENG 505 (Java) — A/A+ (core OOP foundations, collections, and problem-solving)
  • SENG 520 (Software Analysis & Design) — A/A+ (design thinking and structured analysis)

I started programming with Java fundamentals (including ITP 120) and built a baseline in: variables, loops, methods, classes, arrays/ArrayLists, basic I/O, and introductory stacks/queues. Like most people learning to code, some concepts clicked immediately and others required reps. I’m building the problem-solving muscle intentionally now — not just completing assignments.

What I’m Learning Now

To become competitive for full-stack or front-end roles, I’m working through a structured path in modern web development:

  1. HTML & CSS fundamentals (structure, layout, responsiveness)
  2. JavaScript fundamentals + DOM (logic, events, real interactivity)
  3. React (components, state, hooks, building real UIs)
  4. Node + Express (APIs and server-side development)
  5. PostgreSQL (data modeling, queries, CRUD, relationships)

In parallel, I’m returning to Java for data structures and algorithms over time so my core fundamentals match my ambition. I’m not aiming to “finish a course.” I’m aiming to become the type of engineer who can ship features, debug under pressure, and explain decisions clearly.

  • Process discipline: military and corporate environments both punish sloppy execution.
  • Communication: translating complex requirements into clear decisions is a daily skill.

I care about compensation because I’m supporting a family — but I care just as much about the work: building reliable products, learning from strong engineers, and improving every week.
